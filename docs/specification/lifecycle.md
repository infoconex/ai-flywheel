# AI Flywheel Lifecycle

The AI Flywheel operates as a recurring eight-stage cycle:

**Execute → Observe → Evaluate → Classify → Adapt → Validate → Persist → Reuse**

Governance is not an additional lifecycle stage. It is a continuous constraint that applies whenever the Flywheel proposes an action, decision, change, or persistent learning.

For visual models of the runtime and learning relationships, see the [architecture overview](../architecture/README.md).

## Before the Cycle: Human Authorization

A human or authorized governing body establishes the scope within which the Flywheel may operate.

That authorization is represented through a Governance Policy defining autonomous authority, approval requirements, escalation conditions, and prohibited actions.

The human does not need to manually initiate every execution. Once authorized, the Flywheel may operate in response to schedules, events, triggers, requests, or continuous conditions within its delegated authority.

## 1. Execute

The AI performs the work using the current combination of:

- procedural guidance from the SOP,
- AI reasoning for orchestration, interpretation, and judgment,
- and deterministic capabilities for reliable, repeatable operations.

These mechanisms participate together during execution. They are not separate lifecycle stages.

Governance applies throughout execution. When an action crosses an authority boundary or an unresolved uncertainty boundary, the affected decision is escalated.

Where practical, escalation should pause only the affected action rather than unnecessarily stopping unrelated authorized work.

## 2. Observe

The system captures relevant execution evidence, including outputs, errors, tool results, timing, external state, validation signals, human decisions, and other indicators of actual outcome.

Human judgments and approvals are part of the evidence generated by the Flywheel when they materially affect execution or learning.

## 3. Evaluate

The AI compares the observed outcome with the intended outcome and applicable success criteria.

The result should distinguish verified success, partial success, known failure, and uncertain outcome.

An uncertain outcome may itself trigger escalation when the system cannot responsibly determine what occurred.

## 4. Classify

The AI identifies the nature and likely source of any weakness or learning opportunity.

Classification should consider whether the issue belongs primarily to:

- deterministic implementation,
- procedural guidance,
- missing validation,
- missing or incomplete reasoning knowledge,
- runtime reasoning,
- environmental change,
- governance or authority,
- or an incorrect assumption about where responsibility should reside.

This is the stage at which the Flywheel asks:

> **Where should this learning live?**

The answer may be deterministic capability, procedural knowledge, reasoning knowledge, validation, or a proposed governance change.

## 5. Adapt

The system selects the appropriate improvement target and applies or proposes a change.

Possible adaptations include:

- code changes,
- new deterministic tools,
- SOP changes,
- stronger validation,
- new reusable reasoning guidance,
- movement of responsibility across the Moving Determinism Boundary,
- or a proposed Governance Policy change.

Governance determines whether the adaptation may be applied autonomously, requires human approval, requires human judgment, or is prohibited.

The AI may recommend an expansion of its authority but may not grant itself additional authority.

## 6. Validate

The proposed improvement is tested against appropriate evidence before it is trusted for future use.

Validation should be proportional to the risk and nature of the change. A code change may require automated tests. An SOP change may require replay against representative scenarios. Reasoning guidance may require evaluation against known examples.

Human approval does not replace technical or operational validation when validation is otherwise required.

## 7. Persist

Validated and authorized learning is stored in a durable operational asset so it can survive the current context and execution.

Possible persistent assets include deterministic capabilities, SOP changes, validation rules, reasoning guidance, reusable knowledge, and approved Governance Policy changes.

## 8. Reuse

Future executions begin with the improved system. The next cycle therefore starts from a more capable operating state rather than repeating the same discovery from scratch.

A human decision should also be reused when appropriate. The Flywheel should distinguish between a one-time judgment and a reusable lesson that can reduce unnecessary future escalation.

## Governance Outcomes

At any point where governance is relevant, a proposed action or change should resolve to one of four outcomes:

### Authorized

The AI knows what to do and has authority to do it. The Flywheel proceeds autonomously.

### Approval Required

The AI can recommend an action but lacks authority to execute or persist it. The Flywheel requests human permission.

### Judgment Required

The AI lacks sufficient evidence or certainty to responsibly determine the correct action. The Flywheel requests human judgment.

### Prohibited

The Governance Policy does not permit the action. The Flywheel does not execute it unless the Governance Policy itself is changed through an authorized process.

## Compounding Behavior

The Flywheel effect occurs when reuse causes the benefits of previous cycles to accumulate.

The goal is not continuous change for its own sake. Stable successful behavior should remain stable. The system should evolve when evidence demonstrates that a persistent improvement is justified.

A mature Flywheel should reduce repeated human involvement where evidence supports safe delegation while preserving human authority over decisions that should remain human-controlled.
