# Framework Comparison Matrix

> **Research status:** Initial working map. Ratings and scores are hypotheses to be validated against primary sources before publication as factual conclusions.

## Scoring System

The matrix uses a **0–3 similarity score** to represent how closely each framework appears to overlap with each AI Flywheel principle.

- **3 — Strong:** The framework appears to contain a substantially similar mechanism.
- **2 — Partial:** There appears to be meaningful overlap but not full alignment with the AI Flywheel principle.
- **1 — Limited:** The relationship appears indirect, narrow, or only partially relevant.
- **0 — None:** No meaningful overlap has been identified.
- **TBD — Unscored:** Research is not yet sufficient for a responsible score.

A higher score means **greater apparent overlap with the AI Flywheel**, not that one framework is better than another.

Because unresearched areas are excluded rather than scored as zero, totals are shown as:

**points earned / maximum points available for currently scored principles**

The percentage is normalized only across the principles that have been scored. Once all eight principles are scored, the maximum possible total will be **24 points**.

## How to Interpret the Totals

The totals are intended as a **similarity index**, not a novelty verdict.

A framework can achieve a high total score while still differing materially in how the principles are combined, how responsibilities interact, or what role each mechanism plays in the overall operating model.

For example, a high-scoring framework may contain strong equivalents for seven individual principles without combining them into the AI Flywheel's specific model of:

- human-authorized autonomy with explicit authority and uncertainty boundaries,
- deterministic capability, procedural guidance, and AI reasoning as distinct but movable responsibilities,
- and execution evidence that determines where persistent learning should live.

The total score therefore answers:

> **How much of the AI Flywheel appears individually represented in this framework?**

It does **not** by itself answer:

> **Does this framework implement the same overall methodology as the AI Flywheel?**

## Current Interpretation

The matrix suggests substantial prior art for nearly every individual principle already studied. That is expected and should be treated as a strength of the research rather than a problem to hide.

Based on the current provisional scoring, **Metis has the highest apparent overlap at 90.5% of currently scored principles**, followed by GenericAgent at 85.7% and MASFly at 81.0%. These scores should not be treated as final until the underlying framework research has been validated against primary sources.

The human-authority principle remains **TBD** until each framework is reviewed specifically for delegated autonomy, approval requirements, uncertainty escalation, governance over self-modification, and whether the AI can expand its own authority.

The most important unresolved comparison is whether any existing framework explicitly combines:

- human-authorized autonomy with explicit authority and uncertainty boundaries,
- deterministic capability, procedural guidance, and AI reasoning as distinct but movable responsibilities,
- and execution evidence that determines where a persistent improvement should live.

These questions are examined most directly in the [Human Authority dossier](principles/01-human-authority.md), [Moving Determinism Boundary dossier](principles/03-moving-determinism-boundary.md), and [Evolution Routing dossier](principles/06-evolution-routing.md).
